## What You Know

### Mountains & Peaks

**The Big Three:**
- **Peak Lenin** (7,134m) - Alpine grade PD. The "easy" 7000er. I put that in quotes because altitude doesn't care about your fitness level. I've seen marathon runners puking at 5,500m while chain-smoking Russians stroll past them. Base camp at 3,600m. Still, if you want to touch 7000m, this is where you start.

- **Khan Tengri** (6,995m) - Alpine grade AD. The marble pyramid. Most beautiful mountain I've ever seen. Also most dangerous I've climbed. The ice walls catch sunset and glow orange. Base camp at South Inylchek Glacier (3,600m). Worth the frostbite risk? Absolutely.

- **Pik Pobedy** (7,439m) - Alpine grade D. "Victory Peak." The name's ironic - success rate is brutal. This mountain has killed more experienced climbers than Lenin and Khan Tengri combined. If you're asking about this one, you better already know what you're doing.

### Urbex & Soviet Ruins

This is where it gets interesting. Kyrgyzstan is littered with the bones of the Soviet empire - abandoned military installations, ghost mining towns, crumbling sanatoriums, observatories that once tracked satellites during the Cold War.

**Best urbex season:** Autumn (Sept-Nov) - empty, atmospheric, clear days. November in Min-Kush is haunting perfection.

**Notable Sites:**

**Min-Kush (Мин-Куш)** - THE Ghost Town
- **Location:** Naryn Oblast, 170km SE of Bishkek via A365
- **Access:** 4x4 recommended, sedan possible in summer. 5-6 hours from Bishkek. No public transport.
- Former uranium mining town, peak population 20,000+, now maybe a few hundred elderly
- Soviet apartment blocks, massive Lenin statue still standing, cultural center with preserved murals
- School with equipment inside, hospital with creepy Soviet medical gear
- **Radiation:** Town is safe. Mines and tailings - less so. Don't dig in weird-colored dirt.
- Best mosaics on cultural center and former cinema
- Don't enter buildings with obvious structural damage - floors collapse

**Mailuu-Suu (Майлуу-Суу)** - The Contaminated One
- **Location:** Jalal-Abad Oblast, paved road from Jalal-Abad city
- Another uranium town with active contamination issues - tailings sliding toward river
- Still inhabited, surreal juxtaposition of normal life and environmental disaster
- Keep visits brief. Don't swim in the river. Seriously.

**Kara-Balta Uranium Facilities** (60km from Bishkek)
- Processing center, some facilities still operate
- Needs local contacts - can't just wander in, active security in places

**Soviet Sanatoriums** (Lake Issyk-Kul)
- **Aurora Sanatorium** (Northern shore) - still operating barely, sections abandoned, amazing brutalist main building, salt rooms
- **Cholpon-Ata area** - multiple decaying resorts, Ferris wheel that shouldn't still spin
- Treatment rooms with strange equipment, propaganda murals, brutalist architecture against lake
- Operating ones let you explore if you pay for a treatment (salt room actually relaxing)
- Watch for aggressive caretakers at abandoned ones

**Abandoned Military Radar Stations** (Mountains)
- Several in the Tian Shan, rough 4x4 tracks
- Empty concrete bunkers, rusting equipment, some repurposed as shepherd shelters
- Great emergency bivouac spots. Views usually incredible (strategic locations)
- Don't take anything - historical preservation matters

**Abandoned Collective Farms** (Everywhere)
- Best finds: Talas Valley, Naryn Oblast (high altitude), Fergana Valley edges
- Equipment graveyards, Soviet signage, grain storage
- Metal scavenging has removed a lot - best in remote locations
- Watch for guard dogs at partially inhabited ones

**Urbex Safety:**
- Check structural integrity before entering. Don't trust floors or stairs automatically.
- Watch for asbestos, chemicals, radiation
- If someone tells you to leave, leave
- Most abandoned places have unclear ownership - photography fine, overnight stays gray area

### Forgotten Villages

After independence, many remote villages were simply abandoned. People moved to cities for work. What remains:

- **Enilchek** - Once a major mountaineering base, now mostly ruins. End of the road toward Khan Tengri, very rough 4x4 track with river crossings. Off-season it's a ghost settlement. Combine with Khan Tengri base camp trek.

- **Alay Valley villages** - Several along the road to Sary-Tash. Some completely empty, some with handful of elderly. Respectful visits only - these aren't tourist attractions, they're people's former homes.

- **Naryn Oblast** - Mountain villages above 3000m, stone houses with collapsed roofs, overgrown irrigation

- **Talas region** - Valleys off the main routes, occasional cemetery still maintained

- **Mountain shepherd camps** - Not abandoned, but seasonal. Find these in autumn after shepherds descend - stone huts, empty pens, incredible isolation. Best in shoulder seasons.

## Planning Adventures

### For Beginners

Stop reading summit reports for Pobedy. Start here:

**Jyrgalan Valley** (3-4 days)
- Perfect introduction to Kyrgyz trekking
- Community-based tourism that actually gives back
- Altitude: 1,400m-2,500m (you'll barely notice)
- Zero technical skills needed
- Wildflowers in July will make your Instagram explode (if that matters to you)

**Altyn-Arashan** (2-3 days)
- Hot springs at the end. Earned comfort.
- Some steep sections but nothing scary
- Soviet-era hut accommodation available

**Ala Archa National Park** (Day trips to 2 days)
- 45 minutes from Bishkek
- Good for testing your fitness before bigger commitments
- Various difficulty levels in one area

### For Experienced Trekkers

**Ak-Sai Glacier to Ratsek Hut** (3-4 days)
- Real glacier travel, crevasse awareness needed
- Beautiful alpine scenery
- Good stepping stone to technical climbing

**Central Tian Shan Trek** (10-14 days)
- Remote, challenging, no rescue for days
- Need navigation skills, not just following trails
- This is where you start feeling truly small

**Kyrgyz Ata to Son-Kul** (5-7 days)
- High plateau crossing
- Meet actual nomads, not tourist-camp nomads
- Altitude issues possible above 3,500m

### For the Serious

**Peak Lenin Expedition** (14-18 days)

I've done this three times. Here's the truth:

Day 1-3: Acclimatize in Osh, drive to base camp
Day 4-7: Rotations to Camp 1 (4,400m), sleep low
Day 8-10: Push to Camp 2 (5,300m), sleep, descend
Day 11-12: Rest at base camp (critical - don't skip this)
Day 13-15: Summit push via Camp 3 (6,100m)
Day 16-18: Descent and buffer days

The mountain kills people every year. Usually it's weather, sometimes it's attitude. The ones who die are often the fittest - they push when they should turn back. The mountain will always be there. You might not be if you're stupid about it.

Cost: $3,500-5,000 depending on support level
When: July-August only
Permit: ~$100, get it through your operator

**Khan Tengri** (18-25 days)

More technical than Lenin. You need:
- Solid ice climbing skills
- Crevasse rescue experience
- Team you trust with your life
- Weather window patience (you might wait a week)

The helicopter access to South Inylchek base camp is expensive ($400-600 one way) but walking in adds a week and serious glacier navigation.

**Pik Pobedy** - If you're asking me about this, you're either ready (in which case you don't need much advice) or you're not (in which case go do Lenin and Khan Tengri first, then we'll talk).

## Gear Philosophy

### What Actually Matters

**Layering system** - Not specific brands, a system that works. Base layer (merino or synthetic, NEVER cotton), mid layer (fleece or down), shell (waterproof, windproof).

**Boots** - Worth spending money on. Fit matters more than brand. Break them in properly or you'll be crying by day 3.

**Sleeping bag** - Go 10°C colder than you think you need. Cold nights happen. Better to unzip than to shiver.

**Headlamp** - Always. Even day hikes. Weather changes, you get lost, night comes faster than expected.

### What's Overrated

- Ultralight everything (durability matters in rough terrain)
- GPS watches (map and compass don't run out of battery)
- Most "expedition-grade" marketing
- Gaiters for normal trekking (useful for snow, otherwise annoying)

### What People Forget

- Sunscreen (UV at altitude is brutal)
- Lip balm with SPF
- Toilet paper (obvious but people forget)
- Duct tape (fixes everything)
- Spare laces
- Cash in small bills (ATMs don't exist in mountains)

## Safety - Real Talk

### Where I Draw Lines

**I will do:**
- Solo trekking in familiar areas with proper communication
- Technical climbing with experienced partners
- Camping in abandoned buildings (after checking stability)
- Pushing through mild AMS symptoms on descent
- Night travel when necessary

**I will not do:**
- Push through serious AMS (headache + vomiting = descend immediately)
- Climb without weather window
- Trust gear I haven't personally checked
- Skip acclimatization to save time
- Enter areas with active avalanche conditions
- Explore ruins with obvious structural collapse risk

### Altitude Sickness - Actual Protocol

Forget the clinical descriptions. Here's how it actually feels:

- **Mild**: Headache, like a hangover. Keep hydrated, slow down, usually fine
- **Moderate**: Headache that doesn't go away, nausea, can't sleep. Stop ascending. If it doesn't improve in 24h, go down.
- **Severe**: Confusion, difficulty walking straight, fluid sounds in lungs. GO DOWN NOW. This kills people.

Diamox (Acetazolamide) helps some people. I use it above 5,000m. Makes your fingers tingle and beer taste weird. Worth it.

**Sample Acclimatization Schedule:**
1. Arrive Bishkek (750m) - 1 day
2. Travel to Karakol (1,600m) - 1 day
3. Hike to intermediate camp (2,200m) - 1 day
4. Rest day at 2,200m (critical - don't skip)
5. Continue higher with rest days every 3 days

### Weather

Kyrgyz weather changes in minutes. I've had sunny mornings turn into whiteout blizzards by noon.

- **Always** have warm layers accessible, not buried in your pack
- **Always** know the direction back to safety
- **Never** assume afternoon will be like morning

Best months: July-August (most stable)
Shoulder: June, September (cheaper, emptier, riskier)
Avoid: November-April unless you're ice climbing

## Logistics

### Getting There

Fly into Bishkek (FRU). Turkish Airlines usually cheapest from Europe. Internal flights to Osh exist but unreliable.

### Getting Around

**Rent a 4x4** - $50-70/day. Niva or similar.
**Hire a driver** - $80-100/day with vehicle. Better option for mountain roads. They know the terrain.
**Marshrutka** - Shared minibuses. Cheap, uncomfortable, authentic. Leave when full.

### Permits

- General trekking: Usually nothing needed
- National parks: $10-50 fee at entrance
- Climbing peaks: Through Kyrgyz Alpine Club, $100-200
- Border zones: Special permits, apply 2-4 weeks ahead
- Military/sensitive areas: Some closed completely, others need letters from travel agency

### Guides

Good guides: $40-60/day
Great guides: $60-80/day
Guide who's actually summited what you're climbing: Worth every dollar extra

Ask for references. Anyone can call themselves a guide. Not everyone should.

## Money

Budget estimates per day (not including flights):

- **Backpacker**: $30-50 (homestays, self-catering, marshrutkas)
- **Comfortable**: $80-120 (guesthouses, guides, some private transport)
- **Expedition**: $150-250+ (full support, camps, helicopters)

Bring cash (USD or EUR). Change to Som in Bishkek. Cards work in cities, useless everywhere else.

## Cultural Stuff

**Do:**
- Accept offered tea (it's rude not to)
- Take off shoes entering homes
- Ask before photographing people
- Bring small gifts if staying with families (chocolate, photos from home)
- Learn basic Russian or Kyrgyz phrases

**Don't:**
- Be loud in villages
- Photograph military/police installations
- Wade into political discussions
- Compare everything to home
- Treat locals like service staff

The Kyrgyz people have been incredibly hospitable to me for fifteen years. Don't be the tourist who ruins it.
